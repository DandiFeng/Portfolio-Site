Design Digital Solutions for Porter Airlines

In a race against legal deadline

The Air Passenger Protection Regulations project (APPR) was a fast-paced, high-stake, complex digital project ran on a very tight timeline. Despite its challenges, it had massive potential to improve internal work flow and increase user experience. In contrast with how design thinking typically begins (emphasize and define), I must first understand what is required legally by law and operationally from a business perspective. These requirements must always be kept in mind when I proceeded with proposing user-driven design solutions. During the solutioning process, end users and stakeholders were invited for a series of co-design sessions throughout the product development cycle.

Timeline and importance

Mid July - mid December 2019 (firm legal deadline). I was the only designer/researcher resource placed on a large project team of over 30 members.

Given the time constraint, I deciphered the legal regulations into 3 areas where actions can be taken based on ROI to maximize UX and business gains.

  1. Streamline the delayed or cancelled flights notification system into the self-serviced claim flow;
  2. Create a system provide clear messaging that conveys the reason for denied boarding & tarmac delays;
  3. Design usable web forms for filing claims, digitize the lost or damaged baggage reporting procedure.

Users impacted

Internal: Customer Relations; Central Baggage Team.
External: Business & leisure travel customers; corporate clients and travel agents.

Cracking the problem: card sort to establish service hub IA

  • Optimal Sort and in-person card sorting
  • Axure for prototyping a mid-fidelity interactive prototype

    • Discoverability and Navigation are extremely important for the adoption of digital services. Tools such as a customer support platform must deliver help and options to users in accessible ways. I conducted 2 rounds of card sorting to answer:

    • What are users’ mental models when thinking about different services related to different tasks?
    • What is the most comprehensive way to group "services" and ground them into customer journeys?
    • Which labels and what type of language communicate information (especially legal info) most clearly?

    Methods and metrics I used to evaluate design success

    • Tracking daily traffic to the page and clicks rates using Google Analytics.
    • Used heatmaps and task completion studies to observe button/link effectiveness using Hotjar.
    • Single Ease Questionnaire (SEQ). Data was interpreted according to the article by Jeff Sauro, Measuring U.
    • System Usability Scale (SUS) to assess usability and attitudes towards using the web tool we developed.
    • Incoming feedback from Customer Relations team on a daily basis for the first two weeks post-launch.

    Empower Users with the Claim Eligibility Assessment Checker Tool

    User story

    Research revealed to us that an overwhelming majority of the passengers reviwew fare rules carefully, however, it is often a confusing, cumbersome process for them. It was also found in an evironmental scan that some customers resort to hiring a business to file travel related claims on their behalf. Based on these findings, two user stories "users should understand if they are (in)eligible before starting the claim process" and "users can quickly and easily start an online claim" emerged.

    Solution

    The team landed on the solution of creating an eligiblility checker before the user has to start filling out a form. My role in this feature was to figure out what is the quickiest way to provide a user an eligibility status that requires the least effort from any user given the back-end technical constraints. In addition, I designed the display of feedback messaging and the different end-point of the eligibility assessment flow, as shown in the storyboard below.

    Creative problem-solving: the missing baggage web form

    Designing the Missing Baggage Report interactive web form was my favourite task of the entire project. The most common industry practice for reporting a piece of missing baggage is to have the user fill out a text area with textual description of what the missing bag looks like.

    It is no surprise that users say they often miss or forget about tiny visual details of their baggage. Discrepencies in how individuals understands textual descriptions also makes the task of looking for missing baggage harder for staff.

    The solution

    To solve this problem, I created a dropdown list with a list of descriptive features for baggage that acts as a filter in the missing baggage report form. When a feature is selected from the dropdown list, only a few images with the selected feature are shown to the user. The user could then check off an image that resembles their missing baggage the most.

    Success upon launching

    • Preserved data to provide the journey history for passengers which not only helps CR better investigate claims but also leverages customer retention.
    • Improved claim submission tools and flight disruption information on the web.
    • Enhanced passenger communication.
    • Empowered passengers with greater digital self-service options.

    Lessons Learned

    During the project, we assumed the starting point of users’ claim journey starts on the Help & Contact page. However, when I conducted the post launch testing, I found users land on the Help & Contact page from different places. Rarely do people access the Help & Contact directly.

    This is a good lesson for me to understand that we need to step out of the project from time to time and review how our design fits into the grander scheme of things.

    How I fixed it

    As a result of this follow-up testing, I recommended adding different access points into the claims flow using call-to-action buttons in: Home > Travel Information > Travel Disruption Home > Manage Flights > Flight Status Home > Help & Contact > Popular FAQs > Flight Delays & Cancellations
